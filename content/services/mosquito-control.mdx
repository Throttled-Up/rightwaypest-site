<ServiceSection slot="section_1" heading="Why Jacksonville Properties Face Year-Round Mosquito Pressure">

Jacksonville sits in a subtropical zone that receives over 50 inches of rainfall annually. That moisture feeds directly into the conditions mosquitoes need to breed. Duval County's proximity to tidal salt marshes along the Intracoastal Waterway and St. Johns River creates massive larval habitat, while sandy soil depressions across residential yards hold standing water long after storms pass. The result is a breeding season that runs from March through November, with *Aedes aegypti* and *Aedes albopictus* (the Asian tiger mosquito) remaining active even during mild Northeast Florida winters.

These two species are daytime biters that thrive in suburban yards. Both are confirmed vectors for diseases including Zika, dengue, and chikungunya. This is a public health concern, not just a backyard annoyance.

Duval County mosquito control operations treat public waterways and drainage infrastructure, but they do not service private residential properties. That gap between public abatement and your backyard is exactly where professional yard mosquito treatment becomes necessary.

</ServiceSection>

<ServiceSection slot="section_2" heading="Our Mosquito Treatment Process, From Inspection to Barrier">

Every mosquito treatment we perform starts with a thorough yard inspection. We walk the property identifying breeding sites: birdbaths, clogged gutters, saucers beneath potted plants, and low spots in St. Augustine turf where water pools on Jacksonville's compacted sandy soil. Eliminating or treating these sources is half the battle.

For the barrier application, we use a backpack mist blower to apply a residual adulticide to the undersides of foliage, along fence lines, under decks, and into shaded harborage areas where mosquitoes rest during daylight hours. In standing water features and drainage areas that can't be eliminated, we apply BTI (Bacillus thuringiensis israelensis) granules to target larvae before they emerge.

We treat on a 21-day cycle during peak season and adjust the schedule after heavy Northeast Florida rainfall events that create new breeding conditions. Every RightWay technician holds a Florida DACS-issued pest control operator license and applies all products per label requirements under FDACS Chapter 5E-2 regulations.

</ServiceSection>

<ServiceSection slot="section_3" heading="Natural Mosquito Lawn Treatment Options for Jacksonville Yards">

Homeowners in Ponte Vedra, Nocatee, and Palm Valley increasingly ask us for mosquito control that won't harm the pollinators, pets, and Intracoastal waterways surrounding their properties. We offer a natural mosquito lawn treatment program built around three OMRI-listed and NOP-compliant tools:

- Garlic-oil barrier sprays
- Cedar oil-based repellent applications
- BTI biological larvicides that target mosquito larvae in standing water without affecting beneficial insects

Natural products carry a shorter residual life, typically seven to fourteen days compared to twenty-one or more for synthetic barriers, so they require more frequent applications. For many of our clients, that's a worthwhile exchange.

Our UF Master-certified technicians also design hybrid programs that pair mosquito treatments with organic compost topdressing to improve soil structure and drainage in Northeast Florida's sandy soils. Reducing standing water on your property is the single most effective long-term mosquito reduction strategy, and healthier turf makes that possible year-round.

<ServiceSection slot="section_4" heading="Seasonal Mosquito Control Calendar for Northeast Florida">

Jacksonville's subtropical climate means *Aedes albopictus* eggs start hatching as early as March, the moment daytime temperatures consistently reach 70°F. Skipping that first barrier treatment lets the initial generation reproduce unchecked. By June, populations grow exponentially and become far harder to suppress.

**March through April:** We apply the first perimeter barrier treatment to knock down emerging adults before they breed. This is the most critical window of the year.

**May through September:** Peak season requires barrier reapplication on a 21-day cycle. After Jacksonville's heavy afternoon thunderstorms, we reapply larvicide to newly pooled standing water and inspect for breeding sites caused by irrigation leaks or landscape changes.

**October through November:** We reduce treatment frequency but continue monitoring, especially in shaded yards near the St. Johns River, Intracoastal, and drainage ditches where moisture lingers.

**December through February:** Dormant-season yard cleanup removes leaf litter and debris from landscape beds where mosquito eggs overwinter, reducing the population that hatches the following spring.

Consistent year-round management is what separates controlled yards from miserable ones.

</ServiceSection>
