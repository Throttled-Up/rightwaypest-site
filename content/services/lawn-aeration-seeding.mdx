<ServiceSection slot="section_1" heading="Why Jacksonville's Sandy Soils Need Professional Aeration">

Most Jacksonville homeowners assume sandy soil can't compact. That assumption costs them their lawn. Foot traffic, repetitive mowing paths, and the heavy downpours from June through September pound the top two to three inches of Duval and St. Johns County sand into a hard pan that roots can't push through.

St. Augustine grass compounds the problem. In our subtropical humidity, it builds thatch layers exceeding an inch thick, forming a dense mat that sheds water and fertilizer instead of absorbing them. In low-lying neighborhoods like Riverside, San Marco, and Mandarin, a high water table already limits root depth. Add compaction and thatch on top, and the turf slowly suffocates.

Core aeration breaks through that barrier:
- Pulls soil plugs that open channels for oxygen, water, and nutrients
- Restores soil profile so follow-up overseeding and organic compost topdressing actually take hold
- Without it, those inputs sit on the surface and wash away

That restored soil profile is what makes the difference between a lawn that recovers and one that declines year after year.

<ServiceSection slot="section_2" heading="Our Core Aeration Process for St. Augustine and Warm-Season Turf">

Every aeration job starts with a hands-on assessment. We measure thatch depth and test soil compaction using a penetrometer to determine exactly how restricted root growth has become. Once we have those readings, we schedule irrigation 24 to 48 hours before service day so Jacksonville's sandy-clay mix softens enough for clean plug extraction.

We run a drum-style core aerator fitted with hollow tines, pulling 2 to 3 inch plugs on 4 to 6 inch spacing across the entire lawn. Core aeration only. Spike aeration drives metal into the soil without removing material, which actually increases compaction in our local soil profile. For St. Augustine lawns, we adjust tine depth and spacing to protect the stoloniferous runners this grass depends on for lateral spread and recovery.

The extracted plugs stay on the surface. As they break down over the following weeks, they return organic matter and beneficial microbes directly to the soil. Most Jacksonville lots in the 5,000 to 8,000 square foot range take one to two hours to complete.

</ServiceSection>

<ServiceSection slot="section_3" heading="Overseeding and St. Augustine Grass Restoration After Aeration">

St. Augustine, the dominant turf across Jacksonville and St. Johns County, doesn't produce viable commercial seed. That biological reality changes the restoration strategy entirely. After we aerate, bare or thinning areas are restored through plugging, sprigging, or sod patching, giving St. Augustine stolons direct access to loosened soil where they establish faster.

For homeowners who want green turf through Northeast Florida's mild winters, we overseed with certified, weed-free annual ryegrass in October through November. This temporary cover crop thrives in our USDA Zone 9a conditions, then dies back naturally as St. Augustine breaks dormancy in spring. The aeration cores create ideal microsites where seed falls directly into soil contact rather than sitting on top of thatch.

We coordinate every overseeding with our lawn fertilization program, applying a high-phosphorus starter blend timed to feed tender seedlings without burning them. That pairing, mechanical soil preparation plus targeted nutrition, gives new growth the strongest possible foundation in our sandy Northeast Florida soils.

<ServiceSection slot="section_4" heading="Best Time to Aerate Your Jacksonville Lawn">

Jacksonville's soil temperatures stay consistently above 65°F from April through October, but that doesn't mean the entire window is right for aeration. For St. Augustine and other warm-season grasses that dominate Northeast Florida lawns, the prime aeration window falls between April and June, when turf is actively pushing new growth and can close core holes quickly.

Aerating warm-season turf in fall is a costly mistake. As St. Augustine enters dormancy, it can't heal the openings you've just created. Those wounds become entry points for winter weeds like Poa annua, which thrives in Jacksonville's mild December and January temperatures.

One exception: if you're overseeding with annual ryegrass for winter color, light aeration in October or November before seeding helps establish seed-to-soil contact.

Our UF Master-certified technicians don't guess at timing. We use soil thermometers and local weather data to schedule each aeration when your specific turf type is primed for recovery, not just when the calendar says so.
