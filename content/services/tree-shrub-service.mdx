<ServiceSection slot="section_3" heading="Common Tree and Shrub Pests in Northeast Florida">

Asian ambrosia beetles bore into stressed crape myrtles and redbuds, pushing out tiny toothpick-like frass tubes from the bark. By the time you spot them, internal damage is already underway. Lobate lac scale coats hollies and wax myrtles with waxy bumps, producing sooty mold that blackens leaves beneath the colonies. Whiteflies cloud ligustrum and ficus when disturbed, while spiraling whitefly leaves distinctive white spirals on palm fronds. Tea scale turns camellia leaf undersides white with dense insect armor. Chilli thrips stipple Indian hawthorn and rose foliage to a silvered, bronzed texture that homeowners often mistake for drought stress.

Each pest requires different treatment chemistry and timing. Jacksonville's summer humidity and daily afternoon thunderstorms create ideal conditions for fungal and bacterial diseases that destroy ornamental plantings. Entomosporium leaf spot defoliates Indian hawthorn and red-tip photinia. Cercospora leaf spot disfigures crape myrtles. Magnolias develop algal leaf spot in shaded, moisture-heavy canopies. <ServiceSection slot="section_5" heading="Deep Root Fertilization and Soil Health in Sandy Florida Soils">

Jacksonville's sandy soils drain fast, too fast. Surface-applied granular fertilizers wash past the feeder root zone within days of a typical Northeast Florida afternoon rain, leaving hollies, azaleas, and ornamental trees starved despite regular feeding.

We address this with high-pressure liquid deep root fertilization, injecting a balanced blend of macro and micronutrients eight to twelve inches into the soil where feeder roots actually absorb them. Before we formulate, soil testing identifies the exact deficiency profile for your property. Northeast Florida soils are commonly low in iron and manganese, the direct cause of interveinal chlorosis that yellows holly and azalea foliage between the veins.

We pair deep root feeding with organic compost topdressing applied around the drip line. Compost introduces beneficial microbial communities that improve both nutrient uptake and water retention in sandy substrates. This combination builds soil biology over successive seasons, a long-term investment in plant vitality rather than a temporary fix.
