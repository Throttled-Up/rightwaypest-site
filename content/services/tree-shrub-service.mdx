<ServiceSection slot="section_1" heading="Why Jacksonville Trees and Shrubs Need Year-Round Professional Care">

Jacksonville's subtropical humidity drives fungal pathogens year-round. Sooty mold blackens live oak canopies. Cercospora leaf spot defoliates crape myrtles. Entomosporium ravages Indian hawthorn and ligustrum hedges. Duval and St. Johns County soils are sandy with low organic matter, meaning applied nutrients leach quickly and leave azaleas, hollies, viburnum, and palms chronically underfed without a structured fertilization program.

Coastal properties along the Ponte Vedra and Atlantic Beach corridors face the added stress of salt spray burn on tender new growth. Whiteflies colonize ligustrum, scale insects encrust holly branches, spider mites explode on azaleas during dry spells, and ambrosia beetles bore into stressed crape myrtles and oaks. Because Northeast Florida rarely sees a hard freeze, pest populations never fully reset between seasons.

That biological reality makes integrated pest and disease management a twelve-month discipline, not a seasonal afterthought.

<ServiceSection slot="section_3" heading="Common Tree and Shrub Pests in Northeast Florida">

Asian ambrosia beetles bore into stressed crape myrtles and redbuds, pushing out tiny toothpick-like frass tubes from the bark. By the time you spot them, internal damage is already underway. Lobate lac scale coats hollies and wax myrtles with waxy bumps, producing sooty mold that blackens leaves beneath the colonies. Whiteflies cloud ligustrum and ficus when disturbed, while spiraling whitefly leaves distinctive white spirals on palm fronds. Tea scale turns camellia leaf undersides white with dense insect armor. Chilli thrips stipple Indian hawthorn and rose foliage to a silvered, bronzed texture that homeowners often mistake for drought stress.

Each pest requires different treatment chemistry and timing. Misidentification means wasted product and continued damage, which is exactly why our UF Master-certified technicians diagnose before they spray. <ServiceSection slot="section_4" heading="Disease Management for Jacksonville's Humid Climate">

Jacksonville's summer humidity and daily afternoon thunderstorms create ideal conditions for fungal and bacterial diseases that destroy ornamental plantings. Entomosporium leaf spot defoliates Indian hawthorn and red-tip photinia. Cercospora leaf spot disfigures crape myrtles. Magnolias develop algal leaf spot in shaded, moisture-heavy canopies. In low-lying Nocatee and Bartram Park lots with poor drainage, Phytophthora root rot can take out azaleas outright, and Armillaria mushroom root rot colonizes stressed trees sitting in saturated soil.

We treat active infections with appropriate systemic fungicides selected for the specific pathogen identified during inspection. But spraying alone isn't enough. We selectively prune to restore air circulation, adjust irrigation schedules to reduce leaf wetness, and apply organic compost topdressing around root zones to build beneficial soil biology that suppresses soil-borne pathogens.

Florida's rainy season demands tighter treatment intervals than a generic national schedule allows. We compress our spray calendar from June through October to match the actual disease pressure in Duval and St. Johns County.

<ServiceSection slot="section_5" heading="Deep Root Fertilization and Soil Health in Sandy Florida Soils">

Jacksonville's sandy soils drain fast, too fast. Surface-applied granular fertilizers wash past the feeder root zone within days of a typical Northeast Florida afternoon rain, leaving hollies, azaleas, and ornamental trees starved despite regular feeding.

We address this with high-pressure liquid deep root fertilization, injecting a balanced blend of macro and micronutrients eight to twelve inches into the soil where feeder roots actually absorb them. Before we formulate, soil testing identifies the exact deficiency profile for your property. Northeast Florida soils are commonly low in iron and manganese, the direct cause of interveinal chlorosis that yellows holly and azalea foliage between the veins.

We pair deep root feeding with organic compost topdressing applied around the drip line. Compost introduces beneficial microbial communities that improve both nutrient uptake and water retention in sandy substrates. This combination builds soil biology over successive seasons, a long-term investment in plant vitality, not a temporary fix. If your ornamentals are declining despite regular watering, call 904-290-6400 for a soil test and deep root fertilization assessment before another season of nutrient loss compounds the damage.
