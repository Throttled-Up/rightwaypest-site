<ServiceSection slot="section_1" heading="Why Fire Ants Thrive in Jacksonville's Sandy Soil and Subtropical Climate">

Duval County's well-drained sandy soils give red imported fire ants (*Solenopsis invicta*) exactly what they need: loose, easy-to-excavate ground for building extensive tunnel networks and mounds. Pair that with Jacksonville's warm, humid subtropical climate, and colonies operate year-round. Our winters rarely produce sustained freezes, so unlike northern states, there's no seasonal die-off to thin populations.

Florida sits within the USDA-APHIS fire ant quarantine zone, which means the state actively monitors the spread of this invasive species. That's how entrenched the problem is here. Rapid development in St. Johns County areas like Bartram Park and Durbin Crossing makes things worse. Grading, fill dirt, and fresh sod installation disturb existing colonies and scatter queens into new territory, where they establish mounds fast.

St. Augustine grass lawns, the dominant turf across Jacksonville, compound the issue. The warm, irrigated conditions homeowners maintain for healthy St. Augustine are the same conditions fire ants select for mound building.

<ServiceSection slot="section_3" heading="Mound Drenches vs. Broadcast Baits: What Actually Collapses the Colony">

Pouring a drench or boiling water on a fire ant mound affects the workers you can see. That's the problem. The queen sits deep below the surface, untouched. Worse, the disturbance triggers budding, where the colony fractures and satellite queens establish new mounds across your yard. You started with one mound and now have four.

Broadcast bait programs work differently. Foraging workers carry the bait granules back to the colony and pass them to the queen through trophallaxis, direct food transfer. Once the queen dies, the colony collapses.

UF/IFAS Extension research shows broadcast bait applications reduce fire ant populations by 80–90% within four to six weeks, compared to just 30–40% for mound-only treatments. We use this research-backed broadcast approach as the foundation of our fire ant control protocol, pairing it with targeted mound treatments only after the bait has had time to circulate through the colony structure.

<ServiceSection slot="section_4" heading="Protecting Children, Pets, and Irrigation Systems from Fire Ant Damage">

Fire ant stings produce painful pustules that last for days. For sensitive individuals, especially young children, a mass stinging event can trigger anaphylaxis. The Florida Poison Information Center logs thousands of sting-related calls each year across the state. Dogs and outdoor cats face the same danger; colonies swarm exposed paws and muzzles before an animal can retreat.

The damage extends beyond health. Fire ants are drawn to electrical currents and routinely infiltrate irrigation valve boxes, pool pump wiring, and A/C contactors in Jacksonville homes, causing failures that are expensive to repair.

We schedule our fire ant treatments around your household. After granular bait application, children and pets can return to treated areas within two to four hours once the product is watered into the soil. In yards with heavy pet traffic, we use reduced-risk bait formulations that minimize exposure while still collapsing colonies.

</ServiceSection>

<ServiceSection slot="section_5" heading="Seasonal Fire Ant Treatment Calendar for Northeast Florida">

Jacksonville's subtropical climate means fire ants stay active nearly year-round, but their behavior shifts with the seasons, and so should your treatment approach.

**March through May:** Peak mating flight season, when newly mated queens drop from the air and establish fresh colonies across your lawn. This is the ideal window for a broadcast bait application that intercepts colonies before they mature.

**June through August:** Surface temperatures above 95°F push foraging activity deep underground. Mound-specific treatments applied early morning, before ants retreat from the heat, deliver the best contact results.

**September through November:** A second surge as colonies forage aggressively, stockpiling resources ahead of cooler months.

**December through February:** Still demands monitoring. Jacksonville rarely sees sustained freezes, so colonies remain viable below the surface.

A single seasonal treatment leaves gaps. Our bi-monthly pest barrier plan keeps broadcast bait and mound treatments on a consistent rotation, integrating fire ant suppression into your broader lawn and pest management program for continuous, reliable control.
